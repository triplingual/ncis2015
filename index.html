People

Like soylent green, DH is people. My apologies to those for whom that's a spoiler either for the movie or for DH.

And yet. Machines don't do anything scholarly significant by themselves yet. To engage in DH, there has to be another person in the room. To my mind, to engage in meaningful DH you have to address the lives of real humans. To expand human knowledge and to improve humanity, you'll get farther working with people than with computers.

This is not to discount the tools I've mentioned before. But it does mean that the tools were written by people, are supported by people, have been used by people, are interpreted by people, and — most to the point — answer human questions that humans need to push forward using their own brains.

It also means that tools, their intentions, their communities, and their biases reflect, reproduce, and reinforce pre-existing power structures and relations.

Partnering

Or, Independent Scholars Can't Be

I've listed some approaches and some tools that enable those approaches, but you'll get farther faster if you have a team to work with.

One team I'm part of has a photography historian and theorist as a PI, a young scholar with a background in public scholarship as a project co-coordinator, a statistician as the other project co-coordinator, a geographer for the maps, and a Scandinavianist as the implementation lead, to say nothing of the librarians, government staff, and ad hoc consultations that have boosted the project.
